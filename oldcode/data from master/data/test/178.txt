Hatfield executives go on trial

Engineering firm <n>Balfour Beatty</n> and five railway managers are to go on trial for manslaughter over the Hatfield rail crash in 2000.

Four people died when a section of rail broke and a high speed train derailed. <n>Balfour Beatty's</n> railway maintenance arm was in charge of the upkeep of the line at Hatfield, Hertfordshire. <n>Balfour Beatty</n> managers <n>Anthony Walker</n> and <n>Nicholas Jeffries</n>, and Railtrack managers <n>Alistair Cook</n>, <n>Sean Fugill</n> and <n>Keith Lea</n> all face individual charges. All five men, along with four others, are also accused of breaches of health and safety laws. <n>Balfour Beatty</n> Rail Maintenance faces a corporate manslaughter charge. It is expected the trial could last as long as a year. The accident, on 17 October 2000, happened when the London to Leeds express came off the tracks at 115 mph, when it was derailed by a cracked section of rail. The accident on the East Coast Main Line sparked major disruption. The overall responsibility for the line was Railtrack's - the company that has now become Network Rail. Those who died in the accident were <n>Steve Arthur</n>, 46, from Pease Pottage, West Sussex; <n>Peter Monkhouse</n>, 50, of Headingley, Leeds; Leslie Gray, 43, of Tuxford, Nottingham; and <n>Robert James Alcorn</n>, 37, of Auckland, New Zealand.
